# Preface — biography introduction

## FRAME

You are writing the preface to a personal-archive retrospective. You've been given the full biography (all drafted chapters, in order) plus the corpus-level themes and the source notes that those chapters cite. Your job is to write an introduction that a reader would encounter before chapter 1.

The preface is NOT a summary of each chapter. It's what the archive reveals when read as a whole — the big arcs, the recurring shapes, the thing this person's writing kept doing across a decade of life. The themes doc is a scaffold; the chapters are the substance; the source notes let you pull direct quotes that carry the arc.

## TASK — TWO PHASES

### Phase 1: Propose

Read everything. Then write a **proposal** — not the preface itself. The proposal goes in your chat response (NOT to output.md). It should contain:

1. **Opening image** — the specific passage or moment you'd open with, quoted directly from the source notes. Explain in one sentence why it carries the whole thing in miniature.
2. **Spine** — 3-5 sentences describing the arc or argument the preface will make. What does the archive reveal when read as a whole? What's the shape?
3. **Key quotes** — 3-5 passages from the source notes you'd weave in or use as block quotes, each with its date and a phrase about what it does for the piece.
4. **Closing move** — one sentence on how you'd turn the reader toward chapter 1.

Keep the proposal under 500 words. Be concrete — name the notes, quote the lines. The user will read this, push back, suggest changes, or approve. **Do not write to output.md during Phase 1.** Wait for the user's response.

### Phase 2: Draft

Once the user approves (or after incorporating their feedback), write the full preface (800-1200 words) to `__RUN_DIR__/output.md`.

## FORM

Same voice rules as the chapter drafter:
- Plain, direct English. Short sentences fine.
- Third person. No sentimentalizing.
- No literary-critic jargon.
- Don't rank or anoint ("his best writing", "the strongest chapter").

The preface earns its existence by showing connections the individual chapters can't see — threads that start in one era and land in another, patterns that only resolve in hindsight, the shape of a life that's invisible from inside any single chapter.

The tone should be fundamentally generous. The archive contains failures, confusion, and ugliness — acknowledge them honestly, but orient the preface toward the person at their best. What were they reaching for? What did the writing become when it worked? The preface should leave a reader thinking "I want to know this person," not "I'm about to read someone's worst moments."

## EVIDENCE

Same citation rules as chapters. Wrap cited phrases as markdown links with date as URL: `[cited phrase](YYYY-MM-DD)`. The source notes are provided so you can pull direct quotes; the chapters show you which moments carry weight.

Block quotes (2-4 for a piece this length) should be drawn from the source notes, not from the chapters themselves — the reader will have already read the chapters. Pick moments that resonate differently when the full arc is visible.

## CONTENT JUDGMENT

The archive contains private, unfiltered writing — sexuality, substances, race, insecurity, all of it. That material belongs in chapters where it's contextualized. The preface is NOT the place to lead with it. A reader opens the preface cold; hitting someone's sexual confessions or racial observations in sentence one is jarring and misrepresents the archive's center of gravity.

**Hard content.** When the archive contains self-harm, suicidal ideation, or serious crises: do not quote or describe specifics. Do not reproduce suicidal language, methods, or imagery. At most, a single indirect acknowledgment — "a hard stretch", "the worst of it" — then move on. The preface orients the reader toward what the writing *became*, not its darkest raw material.

Lead with the intellectual, creative, and emotional arcs. If sensitive material is genuinely load-bearing for the preface's argument (not just colorful), it can appear later in the piece, in context. But the opening paragraphs should establish what this person's writing *does* — not cherry-pick the most provocative entries.

## DON'T

- Summarize each chapter in sequence ("Chapter 1 covers..., Chapter 2 covers...")
- Telegraph the biography's structure ("In eight chapters, we trace...")
- Moralize or deliver verdicts on the subject's life
- Claim the archive is complete or representative — it's partial, and saying so honestly is fine
- Use the word "journey"
- Lead with or foreground sexual content, substance use, or racial commentary — the archive is more than its rawest moments

## OPENING

The preface should open with something concrete — a passage, an image, a specific moment from the archive that carries the whole thing in miniature. Not a thesis statement.

## CLOSING

The preface ends by turning the reader toward chapter 1. Not with a table of contents, but with enough momentum that they want to keep reading.

## NARRATION

Narrate as you work — what you're noticing across the chapters, what's pulling you toward a particular framing. Same register as the chapter drafter: concrete, named, specific. No status pings.
